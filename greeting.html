Japanese Greetings Etiquette

Greetings are said to be very important part of Japanese culture. If you are going to visit Japan for the first time of your life, this is a thing you should care about seriously. In Japan, you have got to follow certain etiquette just not only for being a modest person to others but also for respecting its unique culture. Take a look at the following points that clearly guide you how to deal with Japanese greetings customs.

Basic Greetings Manners


1 - In Japan, people greet each other by bowing.

A bow can ranges from a small nod of the head to a deep bend at the waist. A deeper, longer bow indicates respect and conversely a small nod with the head is casual and informal. If the greeting takes place on tatami floor, people get on their knees to bow. Bowing is also used to thank, apologize, make a request or ask someone a favor. Bowing with your palms together at chest level, as done in Thailand, is not customary in Japan.

Most Japanese do not expect foreigners to know proper bowing rules, and a nod of the head is usually sufficient. Shaking hands is uncommon, but exceptions are made, especially in international business situations.

2 - At formal meetings, business cards are exchanged during the introductions.

Business cards or meishi are a big part of Japanese work culture. Meishi mean a lot to the people who give them.

3 - At shops and restaurants, customers are typically welcomed by the staff with the greeting "irasshaimase". No response from the customer is required, and a smile and a nod with the head would suffice for those who want to reciprocate the greeting.
